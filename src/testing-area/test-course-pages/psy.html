Introduction to Abnormal Psychology: Evaluating and Assessing Mental Disorders

Overview

In this module, we will delve into the tricky task of discerning between "normal" and "abnormal" behavior, underscoring the profound influence of societal shifts on these distinctions. You will learn about the historical discourse among psychologists as they grapple with defining abnormality. Moreover, you will gain insights into the dynamic evolution of mental disorder assessment, diagnosis, and treatment, prompting thoughtful consideration of societal attitudes toward individuals seeking mental health support.

Learning Objectives

After successful completion of this topic, you will be able to...

  1. Analyze how mental health treatments in the past differ from the treatments available today (CLO #2)
  2. Recognize the different methods used to evaluate and classify mental disorders (CLO #1 and #4)
  3. Describe and discuss various psychological assessment techniques (CLO #1 and #4)
  4. Describe how psychological disorders are defined, as well as the difficulties in doing so (CLO #1 and #4)
  5. Explain modern views on mental illness, including the rationale and impact of deinstitutionalization (CLO #2)

Key Terms

asylum

institution created for the specific purpose of housing people with mental disorders

atypical

describes behaviors, thoughts, or feelings that deviate from the norm

biopsychosocial model

model in which the interaction of biological, psychological, and sociocultural factors is seen as influencing the development of the individual

culture

socially shared beliefs, values, norms, expectations, and practices within a group, community, or society at large

deinstitutionalization

process of closing large asylums and integrating people back into the community where they can be treated locally

dysfunction

disruption of a person’s ability to live their life productively or that impair their relationships, ability to think clearly, communicate with others, hold a job or deal with stressful events

etiology

cause or causes of a psychological disorder

humorism

a belief held by ancient Greek and Roman physicians (and until the 19th century) that an excess or deficiency in any of the four bodily fluids, or humors—blood, black bile, yellow bile, and phlegm—directly affected their health and temperament

hysteria

term used by the ancient Greeks and Egyptians to describe a disorder believed to be caused by a woman’s uterus wandering throughout the body and interfering with other organs (today referred to as functional neurological symptom disorder, or conversion disorder, in which psychological problems are expressed in physical form)
